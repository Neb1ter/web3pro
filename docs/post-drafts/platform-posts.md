## Draft 1: Reddit

**Suggested subreddit title**  
How I’d explain exchange rebates and KYC to a complete beginner in 2026

**Body**

If a friend asked me how to enter crypto from zero today, I wouldn’t start with “which coin should I buy.”

I’d start with 4 things that most beginners mix together:

1. What an exchange actually does  
2. What a wallet is and what it is not  
3. Why KYC exists and when you usually need it  
4. How trading fees quietly eat your account over time

The reason this matters is simple: most people lose clarity before they lose money.

Here’s the clean path I’d give a beginner:

- Step 1: learn the difference between exchange account, wallet, and on-chain address
- Step 2: understand KYC before submitting documents, so you know what gets reviewed
- Step 3: compare exchanges by fees, restrictions, and usability instead of only brand size
- Step 4: use a simulator before placing real trades
- Step 5: only then start with spot, not leveraged products

The fee part is more important than many people think. If you trade often, a “small” difference in fees or rebate structure compounds into a real cost line. That doesn’t mean you should blindly chase a referral link. It means fee structure should be treated like spread, liquidity, and withdrawal rules: part of the decision, not the whole decision.

The KYC part is also underrated. Most failed submissions aren’t mysterious. They usually come down to:

- mismatched document information
- poor lighting or cropped images
- unsupported region or document type
- account history issues

I’ve seen a lot of beginner content online skip these operational details and jump straight into coins, alpha, or leverage. That’s backwards. The boring parts are usually the parts that reduce friction the most.

## Draft 2: Medium

**Title**  
What Complete Beginners Actually Need Before Their First Crypto Trade

**Body**

Most beginner guides in crypto are written from the wrong starting point.

They assume the first question is, “What should I buy?”

In reality, the first questions are usually much more basic:

- What is the difference between an exchange and a wallet?
- Why am I being asked to do KYC?
- Which fees matter and which ones don’t?
- How do I avoid making a permanent mistake in my first week?

These questions do not sound exciting, but they determine whether a new user has a smooth first experience or a confusing one.

## 1. Start with roles, not products

An exchange is where most beginners first buy, sell, deposit, and withdraw.  
A wallet is where asset control becomes more direct and self-custodied.  
These are related, but they are not the same thing.

Many beginners treat them as interchangeable. That creates confusion immediately, especially when they later encounter network selection, withdrawal addresses, or seed phrases.

## 2. KYC is not just a formality

KYC is often treated like an annoying pop-up step, but for beginners it is actually an operational checkpoint.

If you understand the review logic in advance, you reduce:

- failed submissions
- repeated uploads
- unnecessary delays
- account access friction

The most practical way to explain KYC is not through legal theory, but through preparation:

- which document is accepted
- how the image should be taken
- what name consistency matters
- how long review may take
- why a submission gets rejected

## 3. Fees deserve more attention than most people give them

In crypto, users often spend hours researching tokens and almost no time researching fee structure.

That is a mistake.

For active users, cost compounds. Spot fees, futures fees, spread, withdrawal costs, and platform-specific discounts all affect the real account outcome. Referral rebates can reduce part of that cost, but they should be understood as one component in a broader decision model.

The right question is not “How do I get the biggest rebate?”

The better question is:

“Which exchange makes sense for my region, product needs, risk tolerance, and trading frequency, and what is the real total cost?”

## 4. Simulators are underused

A simulator gives beginners something most internet content does not: a way to slow down.

Instead of moving from article to deposit screen to live trade in one jump, a simulator lets users:

- recognize the interface
- understand order types
- feel the difference between spot and leveraged products
- see how quickly mistakes happen

That learning gap matters.

## 5. Good onboarding reduces noise

The best beginner experience is not the one with the most content.  
It is the one with the clearest order.

If I had to reduce crypto onboarding to one practical sequence, it would be:

1. Understand the role of exchanges and wallets  
2. Learn the KYC process before submitting anything  
3. Compare exchanges by actual usage needs  
4. Understand fee structure and rebate disclosures  
5. Practice first, trade later  

That sequence is less exciting than “top 10 coins to buy now,” but it is far more useful.

I’ve been structuring this kind of onboarding logic into Get8 Pro as a Web3 learning and navigation project, with exchange information, KYC guidance, fee/rebate explanation, tools, and risk disclosures organized in one place.

## Draft 3: Mirror

**Title**  
Crypto onboarding should feel more like documentation and less like noise

**Body**

One thing that still surprises me about crypto onboarding is how often it is built like marketing instead of documentation.

Beginners are pushed toward action before they have sequence.

They are shown:

- coins before concepts
- leverage before spot basics
- links before restrictions
- promotions before disclosures

The result is predictable. Confusion gets normalized.

I think a better onboarding stack should do four things clearly:

## Explain the path

Not every user arrives with the same intent.

Some want to learn Web3 from zero.  
Some already trade and only care about reducing fees.  
Some are existing users trying to understand whether an account can be retrofitted into a rebate structure.

A useful site should separate these paths instead of flattening them into one long landing page.

## Disclose the business model

If a site uses official partner or referral links, it should say so clearly.

That does not make the content invalid.  
But the disclosure should appear near the decision point, not hidden in a footer.

## Preserve operational knowledge

KYC, fee rules, region restrictions, and simulator-first learning are not glamorous topics, but they are where a lot of real user friction lives.

That operational knowledge is often more valuable than another generic market thread.

## Make source context visible

Readers should not have to guess:

- who wrote this
- when it was last reviewed
- what kind of source basis it uses
- where interpretation ends and raw fact begins

I’ve been iterating on Get8 Pro with this in mind: less “trust me,” more “here is the path, here are the limits, here is the source basis.”

That feels like a healthier model for crypto education, navigation, and decision support.

## Draft 4: 知乎专栏

**标题**  
第一次接触加密货币，真正该先搞懂的不是买什么币

**正文**

很多新手第一次接触加密货币时，最先问的是：

“现在买什么？”

但从实际体验来看，这往往不是最该先问的问题。

真正更应该先搞懂的，通常是下面这几件事：

## 一、交易所和钱包不是一回事

很多人一开始会把“交易所账户”和“钱包”混在一起理解。

其实它们解决的问题不同：

- 交易所更偏向买卖、充值、提现、法币入口
- 钱包更偏向资产自主管理、链上交互、DApp 使用

如果这个概念一开始就没分清，后面一旦遇到提币网络、地址、助记词、链上确认这些问题，就会特别容易慌。

## 二、KYC 不是走流程，而是一个关键关口

很多人把实名认证当成“点点点上传一下”。

但对新手来说，KYC 其实是最容易卡住体验的一步之一。

常见失败原因通常并不复杂：

- 证件信息不一致
- 照片模糊、反光、裁切不完整
- 地区或证件类型不支持
- 人脸核验环境不合规

如果事先知道审核逻辑，第一次通过率会高很多。

## 三、手续费不是小问题

很多人花大量时间研究行情，却很少认真看手续费结构。

实际上，手续费、点差、提币成本、返佣规则这些因素，长期会直接影响账户结果。

返佣不是唯一的决策标准，但它也不该被忽略。更合理的做法是把它放进完整判断里一起看：

- 这家平台适不适合我的地区
- 我要做的是现货还是合约
- 我的交易频率高不高
- 实际总成本是多少

## 四、模拟交易是很多新手最该补的一步

不少新手从文章直接跳到实盘，这是很危险的。

模拟交易最大的价值，不是让你预测市场，而是帮你熟悉：

- 页面怎么用
- 下单类型是什么
- 现货和杠杆/合约差别在哪里
- 错误操作会怎么发生

先把操作顺序跑通，再上真实资金，体验会稳定很多。

## 五、一个更合理的新手顺序

如果让我给完全新手排一个更顺的路径，我会建议这样：

1. 先搞懂交易所、钱包、链上地址分别是什么  
2. 再了解 KYC 流程和常见退回原因  
3. 然后比较交易所信息、费率和适用场景  
4. 再去看返佣和成本结构  
5. 最后先模拟，再实盘  

很多人觉得“基础内容很慢”，但真正让人少走弯路的，恰恰是这些看起来不刺激的步骤。
